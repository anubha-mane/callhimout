Know the signs
Drug-facilitated sexual assault (DFSA)
HOW TO RECOGNIZE IT. WHAT TO DO.
Perpetrators are increasingly using common prescription sedatives — zolpidem (Ambien), benzodiazepines — because they're readily available and leave the body quickly. Signs: sudden unexplained drowsiness, memory gaps, waking in different clothes or positions, unexplained bruises, your drink tasting different.
01
Tell someone now
Don't be alone. Get to a safe place immediately.
02
Go to hospital
Many drugs leave the body within 12–72 hours. Time is critical.
03
Don't shower
Preserve evidence if you can. Don't change clothes.
04
Report to police
Keep any suspected drink or substance for testing.
Source: CNN As Equals investigation · Stanford University · Michel Cramer Bornemann MD

Why we exist

THE MISSION

We built this the week the CNN story broke. Not because we were surprised. Because we were done scrolling past it.

BUILT WITH
RAGE.
AND LOVE.

In April 2026, CNN's As Equals team published a months-long investigation into a global network of men who drug and assault their partners, then share and sell content online. One website had 62 million visits in a single month. The platforms did nothing. Governments moved slowly.

We built Call Him Out because women needed a tool that worked now, not after the next hearing. A place to report without being disbelieved. To warn each other before a date. To see that they are not alone — that this is not personal failure, it is a systemic emergency.

We built it on Telegram because it's already where women are. Because it works globally without an app download. Because it means you can report in 90 seconds from a locked bathroom if you have to.

The website is the public record. Every report feeds it. Every number on the dashboard is a woman who refused to be silent.

The CNN investigation that started this

CNN As Equals investigated a global network of men who use online platforms to share advice on drugging partners, trade videos of assault, and buy and sell "sleeping liquids." They found chat groups with dosage tips, men livestreaming assaults for $20 a viewer with cryptocurrency payment, and a website with 62 million visits in February 2026 alone — whose core audience is in the United States.

Survivors Zoe Watts, Amanda Stanhope, and Valentina shared their stories. All three were abused by intimate partners. All three were disbelieved at some point — by police, by family, by themselves. None of this is rare. The data says so.
